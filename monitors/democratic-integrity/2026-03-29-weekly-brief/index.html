World Democracy Monitor — 24 March 2026

V-Dem 2026 quantifies the US democratic collapse; CPAC Budapest formalises the global far-right network; anti-NGO legislative wave reaches Latin America and Africa.

Ten analytical threads stand out from the week of 24 March 2026.

1. V-Dem 2026 Quantifies the US Collapse

The Democracy Report (published March 17) provides the most authoritative single data point of the week: the United States’ Liberal Democracy Index fell from 0.75 to 0.57 in one year — a 24% decline, the largest annual drop in US history, dropping the world’s most influential democracy from 20th to 51st in global rankings. Severity score: 5.5↑. The report explicitly identifies six of the ten new autocratizing countries as being in Europe and North America. V-Dem’s Lindberg: “several of these countries have the economic and political weight to reshape international organisations, norms, and trade, effectively reshaping the global order.”

2. CPAC Hungary 2026 Formalises the Global Network

The fifth edition (March 21, Budapest) brought together 3,000+ participants from 51 countries. Key development beyond the headline speakers (Orbán, Milei, Weidel, Abascal): Georgian Dream PM Kobakhidze delivered a keynote — formally integrating a government that OSCE has confirmed is conducting torture-level repression into the global far-right legitimacy circuit. Trump addressed by video. A Republicans for National Renewal (RNR) investigation (Washington Examiner, March 11) adds structural context: a US-based group has spent four years systematically bridging MAGA Republicans with Kremlin-adjacent European parties (AfD, RN, FPÖ, SMER, AKP, Georgian Dream), with travel apparently covered by foreign entities.

3. The Anti-NGO Legislative Wave Reaches a New Continent

Amnesty International’s March 23 report documents a coordinated offensive across six Latin American states (Ecuador, El Salvador, Nicaragua, Paraguay, Peru, Venezuela) using an identical legal architecture: stigmatising labels, prior authorisation requirements, punitive foreign funding taxes. The same week, Uganda’s government labelled civil society organisations “terrorist-linked” and proposed an NGO Funding Regulation Framework. The Legislative Watch now tracks 10 active bills and executive actions across 7 countries.

4. Media Capture: Three Simultaneous US Mechanisms

The United States is now operating all three phases of the capture playbook concurrently: Phase 1 (government outlet capture — VOA, RFE/RL, Stars and Stripes); Phase 2 (oligarchic editorial retreat — Bezos/Washington Post laid off 300 of 800 journalists in February); Phase 3 (regulatory weaponisation — FCC Chair Carr threatening licence revocations over Iran war coverage, CBS settling a $16m lawsuit under merger-approval pressure). Brookings identifies the FCC technique as specifically designed to avoid judicial review.

5. Georgia Crosses the Torture Threshold — and Joins the CPAC Circuit

The OSCE Moscow Mechanism rapporteur (March 12) found that repression of protesters and journalists in Georgia “arguably reaches the threshold of torture.” Severity score: 8.0↑. Bank accounts of nine major CSOs frozen under a “sabotage” investigation. Opposition party bans are imminent. The simultaneous appearance of PM Kobakhidze at CPAC Hungary 2026 reveals the strategic logic: Georgian Dream is not just repressing domestically but actively seeking international legitimacy from the far-right network.

6. Iran’s January 2026 Massacres Remain the Most Severe Repression Event

Severity score: 10.0→. Ministry of Health internal estimates of 30,000+ killed in the first 48 hours of the January 8–9 crackdown independently corroborated by HRANA (24,669+ arrested, 52 executions in first 10 days). New protest waves in western Iran (February 16) and universities (February 21). The internet kill-switch deployed on January 8 is now being studied by other authoritarian states as a scalable “digital curfew” model — Iran’s repression is simultaneously a crisis and an export.

7. Venezuela’s Transition Is a Critical Friction Case

Official figures claim 626+ political prisoners released; the UN Fact-Finding Mission (March 12) confirms 87 politically motivated arrests since January 3. Foro Penal confirmed only 91 releases under the February 19 amnesty law. Severity score: 7.0↑ within the Recovery tier. Trump has prioritised oil access over democratic conditions, removing the primary external pressure mechanism.

8. Hungary April 12 Election Is the Most Consequential in Europe This Year

Severity score: 5.0→. CSIS (March 11): first real possibility of Fidesz electoral defeat in 16 years. Polls divergent — Tisza polling 16–20 points ahead in opposition-aligned surveys; electoral redistricting requires Tisza to win by 3–5 percentage points nationally. ODIHR observation mission deployed. IPI (March 17): journalists from HVG and Telex physically ejected from Fidesz campaign events.

9. The Watch List Reveals Three Distinct Entry Mechanisms

The six Watch List countries (Italy, UK, Bangladesh, Israel, India, Mexico) reached the watch threshold via different routes: structural drift (Italy, UK per V-Dem 2026); institutional rupture (Bangladesh); judicial reform (Israel); NDTV/Adani acquisition (India); judicial reform and journalist impunity (Mexico). Five different pathways to the same destination.

10. The Severity Gradient Exposes the Resilience Paradox

The monitor’s 1–10 severity scores reveal a counterintuitive finding: the United States (5.5) and Hungary (5.0) score lower than Nicaragua (9.5), Tunisia (8.5), and Georgia (8.0) — not because the US and Hungarian trajectories are less alarming, but because functional resilience mechanisms are still operational. The severity rubric distinguishes between the depth of decay and the speed of decay. The US and Hungary are at an earlier stage of the playbook but moving faster through it than any other case in modern democratic history.

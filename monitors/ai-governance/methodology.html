Why These Sources

AGM uses a three-tier source hierarchy designed to prioritise primary evidence over press coverage of primary evidence.

Tier 1 — Always Use (Primary Sources)

Lab research blogs (OpenAI, Anthropic, DeepMind, Mistral, xAI, Meta AI), arXiv and bioRxiv preprints, official regulatory texts (EU Official Journal, Federal Register, UK SI), court filings (PACER), government gazettes, official changelogs and technical specifications.

Links go directly to the primary source, never to press coverage of it.

Tier 2 — Use When No Tier 1 Exists

Reuters, Bloomberg, Financial Times, The Information, Import AI, AI Snake Oil, Lawfare, RAND, Brookings, Chatham House, IISS, RUSI. These are used only when no Tier 1 source is available for a given item.

Tier 3 — Last Resort, Always Flagged

General tech press (The Verge, Wired, TechCrunch, Ars Technica). Any item sourced from Tier 3 is flagged with ⚠️ in the report.

Source Domains of Particular Focus

  • arXiv: cs.AI, cs.CL, cs.LG, stat.ML — monitored continuously for capability threshold papers and alignment research
  • SEC filings: S-1, 13F, 8-K — for investment structure, governance, and M&A signals
  • Regulatory texts: EU Official Journal, Federal Register, UK SI database, congressional records (congress.gov)
  • Lab safety documents: OpenAI Preparedness Scorecards, Anthropic RSP threshold triggers, DeepMind programme updates
  • Standards bodies: CEN-CENELEC JTC21, NIST AI RMF, ISO/IEC JTC 1/SC 42

What Asymmetric Flags Mean

An asymmetric flag appears on items where the strategic significance of an event exceeds its mainstream press coverage — or where mainstream framing obscures the structural implication.

Asymmetric flags serve three purposes:

  1. Reframing: Mainstream narrative identifies X. Asymmetric framing identifies what X actually signals for power, regulation, or capability trajectory.
  2. Underweighting correction: An item received less mainstream coverage than its strategic importance warrants. The asymmetric note explains why it matters more than the press suggested.
  3. Structural implication: An apparently routine development (a terminology committee decision, a procurement contract, a standards delay) has a structural consequence that accumulates across issues.

Asymmetric flags are present on Executive Insight (M01) items, model entries in M02, investment items in M03, and most items in M12–M15. They are not editorial opinion — they reflect a direct reading of primary source evidence applied to the strategic question: what does this mean for the distribution of power, capability, and accountability in AI?

What the Country Grid Is

The Country Grid is a weekly snapshot of AI governance signal activity by jurisdiction. It appears in M09 (Law & Litigation) and on the Dashboard.

Each row represents a country or jurisdiction with an active AI governance development in the current issue window. The "signal" column summarises the most materially significant development — a new law, a court ruling, a regulatory enforcement action, a standards publication, or a governance body announcement.

The Country Grid is not a comprehensive regulatory tracker. It reflects only developments that reached the Tier 1 or Tier 2 source threshold within the 7-day window. Absence from the grid does not indicate no activity — it indicates no activity that met the inclusion threshold.

The grid is useful for tracking which jurisdictions are accelerating regulatory activity and which are in enforcement vacuums. It is read alongside the EU AI Act 7-layer tracker (M09) and the Risk Indicators (M07).

Methodology

What the FIMI & Cognitive Warfare Monitor tracks, which source organisations it uses, what the MF1–MF4 filters mean, and what F-flags represent.

What FCW Tracks

The FIMI & Cognitive Warfare Monitor tracks Foreign Information Manipulation and Interference (FIMI) and cognitive warfare operations as a global, multi-actor doctrine. The EEAS defines FIMI as "a pattern of behaviour that threatens or has the potential to negatively impact values, procedures, and political processes" — a definition this monitor adopts with one material extension: all state and near-state actors are in scope, not only Russia and China.

The monitor covers six analytical domains:

  • Active campaigns — discrete, attributed information operations with identified infrastructure, objectives, and actor fingerprints. Tracked from first documented activity through disruption or conclusion.
  • Actor doctrine profiles — persistent profiles of state and near-state actors, documenting their doctrinal frameworks, primary infrastructure, and operational patterns across issues.
  • Platform enforcement environment — systematic tracking of Coordinated Inauthentic Behaviour (CIB) takedowns, Threat Analysis Group (TAG) disruptions, and enforcement gaps across Meta, Google, X, Telegram, and other platforms.
  • Attribution log — regulatory developments, institutional reports, and legal proceedings relevant to FIMI attribution and governance.
  • Cognitive warfare — operations explicitly designed to degrade cognitive capacity, social cohesion, or decision-making rather than to advance specific informational objectives. Includes commercial operators (Black Cube, NSO Group, Psy-Group legacy).
  • Cross-monitor flags — linkages to sister monitors (World Democracy Monitor, European Strategic Autonomy Monitor, AI Governance Monitor) where FIMI operations produce documented effects in those domains.
Why EEAS, DFRLab, and SIO

FCW draws on a curated set of institutional and investigative sources with established methodologies, public transparency, and documented track records on attribution:

  • EEAS (European External Action Service) — EU institutional authority on FIMI. The EEAS FIMI framework and annual threat reports provide the definitional and taxonomic baseline. Limitation: formal mandate restricts attribution to Russia and China; Gulf states, US-linked actors, and commercial operators are outside EEAS scope.
  • DFRLab (Digital Forensic Research Lab, Atlantic Council) — leading open-source intelligence and attribution unit for platform-level information operations. DFRLab reports provide primary-source campaign documentation across multiple actors.
  • SIO (Stanford Internet Observatory) — leading academic research unit on influence operations. SIO investigations provide peer-reviewed-quality attribution for complex operations, particularly those exploiting platform amplification dynamics.
  • Google Threat Analysis Group (TAG) — platform-level disruption reports providing primary-source documentation of Chinese and other nation-state operations.
  • Meta Coordinated Inauthentic Behaviour (CIB) reports — quarterly platform enforcement documentation. Primary source for Doppelganger network and related operations.
  • ASPI ICPC (Australian Strategic Policy Institute, International Cyber Policy Centre) — primary source on Chinese UFWD and PLA operations in the Indo-Pacific.
  • Investigative journalism — Forbidden Stories, OCCRP, Haaretz, Süddeutsche Zeitung, and Politico EU for Gulf state, Israeli, and convergence actor operations where institutional reports are structurally constrained.
What MF1–MF4 Filters Mean

FCW applies four media filter categories (MF1–MF4) to classify the relationship between an information operation and the mainstream media ecosystem. These categories describe the operational context of an item — they do not describe how the analytical pipeline selects or prioritises items.

  • MF1 — Mainstream-covered, confirmed attribution: Operations that have received significant mainstream media coverage and carry confirmed institutional attribution (EEAS, TAG, CIB, or equivalent). Example: Doppelganger network post-Meta CIB action.
  • MF2 — Specialist-covered, confirmed or high-confidence attribution: Operations documented by specialist outlets (DFRLab, SIO, Bellingcat) with strong attribution but limited mainstream reach. Example: ASPI ICPC documentation of UFWD Pacific Island operations.
  • MF3 — Underreported, attributed or assessed: Operations with credible attribution from primary sources that have not received proportionate mainstream coverage. Structural underreporting is itself analytically significant. Example: Gulf state lobbying-to-influence operations in UK Parliament.
  • MF4 — Structurally invisible: Operations that fall outside the attribution mandate of all major institutional monitors (EEAS, TAG, CIB) due to actor, geography, or methodology. These require investigative journalism or academic research for primary documentation. Example: Commercial cognitive warfare operations where state client is unattributed.
What F-Flags Mean

F-flags mark analytically significant features of an operation or the information environment. They appear on the lead signal and individual campaigns to draw attention to properties that change the analytical interpretation of an event.

  • F1 — AI-assisted operation: The operation employs AI-generated or AI-assisted content (synthetic media, LLM-generated text at scale, AI-powered micro-targeting). EEAS 4th FIMI Report (March 2026) found 47% of tracked operations now carry this flag.
  • F2 — Convergence actor: The operation involves an actor that blurs conventional attribution categories — typically US-registered entities with documented infrastructure links to Kremlin-aligned networks, or commercial operators with unattributed state clients. Standard single-actor frameworks cannot capture convergence actors without producing systematic blind spots.
  • F3 — Platform enforcement gap: The operation is operating in or has exploited a documented platform enforcement gap. Specifically relevant to X (88% of EEAS-flagged content remains live) and Telegram (near-zero proactive enforcement). Structural feature, not an anomalous enforcement cycle.
  • F4 — Electoral targeting: The operation is actively targeting an electoral event — campaign narrative, voter perception, or electoral infrastructure. Elevated priority flag. Temporal window: operations flagged F4 within 60 days of a scheduled election date.
